A Brief History of Elections

Elections have been a part of American history since September 13, 1778, when the Confederation Congress passed an ordinace related to elections, which established dates for choosing electors, electing presidents, and the begining of a new government under Our Constitution. Shortly after the first election of the United States was held on Monday, December 15, 1788 where George Washington was elected for his first of his two terms as president, and John Adams as vice president.

As for Chicago, Our city has been holding their mayoral elections ever since Chicago was incorporated as a city in 1837. The first mayor of Chicago was elected in 1837, his name was William B. Ogden. Ogden's term lasted only a year, as from 1838 through 1875, mayoral term lengths were only 1 year long. Interestingly, mayoral elections were held every year from 1838 through 1875, even though their dates were modified several times throughout history.

Importance of Voting

The most recent Municipal Runoff Election was held in 2019. Based on the statistics provided by chicagoelections.gov, the 2019 Municipal Runoff Election had the worst turnout in elecetions in recent history. A turnout is definded to be the participation rate of a given election based on the amount of registered, eligible, or of-age voters. The turnout for the 2019 Municipal Runoffs Election was 33.08%, with 1,592,658 registered voters and with 526,886 ballot casts. Just 1/3 of Chicago's eligible voters were participating in this election. The poor involvement of the other 2/3 of the registered voters do not represent the city of Chicago very well by skipping out voting, and minimizing chances for a genuine change in the city.

Your vote matters! Do not make the mistake of avoiding or forgetting to vote. Your vote may not directly elect the president, but if your vote joins enough others in your voting district or county, your vote undoubtedly matters when it comes to electoral results. Most states have a “winner take all” system where the popular vote winner gets the state’s electoral votes. Do not rely on faith, confidence, or trust from your desired candidate to win. Do not rely on your friends, family or aquintances, to vote for your candidate. Rely on your voice, your support for your candidate, and your action to vote to ensure your candidate, whether it is president, gorvernor, mayor, or alderman, win their election. When you vote, you can proudly say, "I joined this representative's community, and helped in securing the vicotry of this election."
